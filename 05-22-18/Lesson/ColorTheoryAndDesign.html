History of Color Theory:

Sir Isaac Newton

  • Invented the first color wheel.
  • Used prisms to split sunlight and recorded the generated colors:
    • red, orange, yellow, green, Cyan, blue
  • Joined them in a circle to show the natural progression of color.

Johann Wolfgang Goethe

  • Writer and scientist, best known for Faust
  • Opposed Newton's "analytic" approach to color.
  • First to study physiological effects of color and how colors complement or contradict each other.

Johannes Itten

  • Developed current color theory based on material characteristics, composition, and color.
  • Seven types of color contrast:
    • Hue, Value, Temperature, Complement, Simultaneous, Saturation, Extension
    • Reference: UT Dallas
  • Created the "color sphere" based on red/yellow/blue as primary colors.

Color Schemes –

Definitions:

  • Color – The property possessed by an object of producing different sensations on the eye as a result of the way the object reflects or emits light.
    • Also, a general term encompassing all color-related elements in design.
  • Hue – The dominant family of a color (i.e. red, orange, green).
    • White, black, and gray are not hues.
    • Hues can be mixed to create other hues (per the color sphere).
  • Tint – A hue or mixture of hues with white added.
    • Accomplished in digital design through increasing the lightness value.
  • Shade – A hue or mixture of hues with black added.
    • Accomplished in digital design through decreasing the lightness value.
  • Tone – A hue or mixture of hues with gray added.
    • Accomplished in digital design through decreasing the saturation value.

Schemes:

  • Generally created using two, three, or four hues.
  • Use of a color wheel helps determine the most harmonious matches/combinations.
    • Color theory only analyzes hues, but similar approaches can be taken to build a color scheme/palette using tints, shades, and/or tones.
  • Types: (Need a palette to start with? https://coolors.co/app
    • Achromatic – Black, white, or gray
    • Monochromatic – Variations in lightness and saturation of a single color.
    • Neutral – Brown (two complementary hues mixed together) and related variations in lightness and saturation.
    • Analogous – Adjacent colors with one dominant.
    • Complementary – Opposite colors (high-contrast).
    • Near Complementary – Almost opposite colors (i.e. red and blue-green).
    • Split Complementary (analogous complementary) – Complementary variation: Opposite colors with adjacent colors on one side of the wheel.
    • Triadic – Three colors equally spaced (equidistant/triangle spacing).
    • Tetradic (Double/Dual Complementary) – Two complimentary color pairs (rectangle or square spacing, best with one dominant color).
